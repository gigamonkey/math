Irrationality of non-perfect square roots

Theorem. Let $n$ be a positive integer that is not a perfect square. Then $\sqrt{n}$ is irrational.

Proof. Suppose for contradiction that $\sqrt{n} = \frac{a}{b}$ with $a, b \in \mathbb{Z}$, $b \neq 0$, and $\gcd(a, b) = 1$. Then $n b^2 = a^2$, so $n \mid a^2$.

Since $n$ is not a perfect square, write $n = m^2 d$ where $d > 1$ is square-free. Then $d \mid a^2 \Rightarrow d \mid a$ (since $d$ is square-free). Let $c = a/d$ and substitute:

$$m^2 d \cdot b^2 = (cd)^2 \quad \Longrightarrow \quad m^2 b^2 = d c^2$$

Let $p$ be a prime factor of $d$. Since $p \mid m^2 b^2$, we have $p \mid m$ or $p \mid b$, so the $p$-adic valuation of the left-hand side is even. But on the right-hand side, $d$ is square-free, contributing exactly one factor of $p$, while $c^2$ contributes an even number. Thus the $p$-adic valuation of the right-hand side is odd.

This is a contradiction, so $\sqrt{n} \notin \mathbb{Q}$.

$\blacksquare$
