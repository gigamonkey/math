Irrationality of roots of non-perfect powers

Theorem. Let $m$ be a positive integer $\notin \{k^n \mid k \in \mathbb{Z}\}$ and $n \in \mathbb{Z}_{\ge 2}$. Then $\sqrt[n]{m}$ is irrational.

Proof. Suppose for contradiction that $\sqrt[n]{m} = \frac{a}{b}$ with $a, b \in \mathbb{Z}$, $b \neq 0$.

Note that because $m \notin \{k^n \mid k \in \mathbb{Z}\}$ there must be at least one prime factor of $m$, $p$, where $n \nmid \nu_p(m)$. Let $q = \nu_p(m)$.

Rewrite our assumption as $b^n m = a^n$. This gives us $\nu_p(b^n m) = \nu_p(a^n)$. And $\nu_p(a^n) = n \cdot \nu_p(a)$, i.e. $n \mid \nu_p(a^n)$.

However, $\nu_p(b^n) = n \cdot \nu_p(b)$ so $\nu_p(b^nm) = nk + q$ where $k = \nu_p(b)$.

Since $n \nmid q$, $n \nmid (nk + q)$. So we have $b^n m = a^n$ and yet $n \nmid \nu_p(b^n m)$ while $n \mid \nu_p(a^n)$. Thus we have a contradiction and $\sqrt[n]{m}$ must be irrational.

$\blacksquare$
